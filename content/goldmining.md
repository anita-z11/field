# Chinese mining companies have arrived. How will Colombian indigenous peoples protect their land?

<br>

In northwestern Colombia, at the juncture of the Caribbean Sea and the Paciﬁc Ocean, the Atrato River is intertwined with people's lives. Heading south from Quibudo, the capital of Choco province, the river leads you to a small town called Lloro, where the road disappears, and you must continue on foot.

This is one of the wettest places on Earth, with rain almost every day. In summer, the river is a dense, forest-like green. Small streams of clay or gold rise and fall in the turbulent currents, which conceal enormous bodies of water, ﬂowing through mountains and forests to reach the riverside community of COCOMOPOCA. Here, 60 to 100 families live, the vast majority of whom are of Afro-Colombian descent. Centuries ago, their ancestors were brought here as slaves by the Spanish during the colonial period and settled there. These communities have inherited some African traditions while blending with mainstream Colombian culture, creating a vibrant and optimistic culture.

“I’ve always felt that it’s one of the best places in Colombia. There’s so much folk music, dance, and people’s lives are so deeply intertwined with the land,” says Viviana Gonzales, a lawyer from Bogotá who has represented COCOMOPOCA in numerous rights lawsuits. “But at the same time, there’s a fear. You could call it a permanent vulnerability. People live very happy lives, but despair is always there.”

Because gold lies buried beneath the mountains. Choco Province is Colombia's largest goldproducing region, located at the junction of Panama and South America, straddling the "gold" route of the Americas' drug traﬃcking network. Since the 1980s and 90s, various armed groups have long occupied this area, vying for resources. In the early 2000s, multinational corporations began investing here, altering the landscape of mountains and rivers on a scale far exceeding traditional mining methods. At the end of this river, not far from the last village at its deepest point, a gold mine changed hands several times before ﬁnally entering the hands of Zijin Mining, a leading Chinese mining company, in 2020.

However, the project has faced community resistance and has yet to begin construction. Similar stories are unfolding worldwide. Indigenous communities have long been at the forefront of protecting nature, resisting the encroachment of the mining industry on their lands, and leveraging climate change as a global issue. Now, this action is gaining more international recognition, while Chinese companies are increasingly ﬁnding themselves on the side of resistance.
